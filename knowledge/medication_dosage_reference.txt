AfyaPlus Health — Medication Formulation and Dosage-Calculation Reference
(Synthetic reference document for capstone RAG grounding. Not real clinical
guidance. Does not replace the separate Triage Engine component.)

1. Purpose and Scope

This document lists standard commercial formulation concentrations for
medications commonly referenced in AfyaPlus clinical-routing conversations,
so that a qualified clinician who has already decided both the drug and the
dose can look up its available concentration and use AfyaPlus's validated
calculator tool to convert that dose into an administration volume.

This document does NOT recommend which drug to use, what dose is
appropriate for a given patient, or how a dose should be adjusted for age,
weight, renal function, or any other clinical factor. Those determinations
must be made by a qualified clinician using accepted clinical references
and professional judgment outside this system. AfyaPlus's role is limited
to arithmetic: given a dose in milligrams and a concentration in milligrams
per millilitre that a clinician has already supplied, the calculator tool
returns the corresponding volume in millilitres. See Section 7 for
questions this system will not answer.

2. How to Use This Reference With the Calculator Tool

The `calculate_medication_volume` tool takes exactly two clinician-supplied
numeric inputs — `prescribed_dose_mg` and `concentration_mg_per_ml` — and
returns the volume to administer. A typical workflow is:

1. The clinician decides the drug and the dose in milligrams (not sourced
   from this system).
2. The clinician confirms which commercial concentration of that drug is
   in stock, either from this reference or the product label in hand —
   concentrations vary by manufacturer and market, so the label always
   takes precedence over this document if they differ.
3. AfyaPlus's calculator tool divides the dose by the concentration to
   return the volume in millilitres.

Example: a clinician has already decided on a 250 mg dose of a medication
supplied at 125 mg/5 mL (i.e. 25 mg/mL). The tool computes 250 / 25 = 10 mL.
AfyaPlus never selects the 250 mg figure itself — that number must already
come from the clinician.

3. Oral Liquid Formulations (Common Concentrations)

- Amoxicillin oral suspension: commonly supplied at 125 mg/5 mL or
  250 mg/5 mL.
- Paracetamol (acetaminophen) oral suspension: commonly supplied at
  120 mg/5 mL or 250 mg/5 mL.
- Ibuprofen oral suspension: commonly supplied at 100 mg/5 mL.
- Amoxicillin-clavulanate oral suspension: commonly supplied at
  125 mg/31.25 mg per 5 mL or 250 mg/62.5 mg per 5 mL (the clavulanate
  component is not covered by AfyaPlus's calculator tool, which supports
  single-active-ingredient volume calculations only).
- Azithromycin oral suspension: commonly supplied at 200 mg/5 mL.
- Cefixime oral suspension: commonly supplied at 100 mg/5 mL.
- Metronidazole oral suspension: commonly supplied at 200 mg/5 mL.
- Oral rehydration salts (ORS): supplied as a fixed-sachet formulation
  reconstituted in a fixed volume of water per the manufacturer's
  instructions; this is a reconstitution ratio, not a dose-to-volume
  calculation, and is outside the calculator tool's scope.

4. Injectable Formulations (Common Concentrations)

- Benzylpenicillin (crystalline penicillin): commonly reconstituted to
  1,000,000 IU per vial; concentration after reconstitution depends on the
  diluent volume used and must be confirmed against the product insert
  before any volume calculation.
- Ceftriaxone: commonly reconstituted to 1 g per 10 mL (100 mg/mL) for
  intramuscular use, or per the product insert for intravenous use.
- Gentamicin injection: commonly supplied at 40 mg/mL or 80 mg/2 mL.
- Diclofenac injection: commonly supplied at 25 mg/mL (75 mg/3 mL).
- Dexamethasone injection: commonly supplied at 4 mg/mL.

Injectable reconstitution and administration carry higher risk than oral
liquids; AfyaPlus's calculator tool performs the same dose-to-volume
arithmetic for injectables as for oral liquids, but reconstitution steps,
diluent choice, and infusion rate are outside its scope and remain entirely
a clinical decision.

5. Units Reference

- mg = milligram (dose unit this system's calculator tool expects).
- mL = millilitre (volume unit this system's calculator tool returns).
- mg/mL = concentration, i.e. milligrams of active drug per millilitre of
  formulation — the second input the calculator tool expects.
- IU = international unit, used for some biologics and antibiotics (e.g.
  benzylpenicillin); IU-based products must be converted to an mg/mL or
  equivalent concentration figure from the product insert before use with
  this system's calculator tool, which operates on mass-based
  concentrations only.

6. Storage Reference

Most oral liquid antibiotic suspensions listed in Section 3 are dispensed
as a dry powder reconstituted with water at the pharmacy and require
refrigeration (2-8°C) after reconstitution, with a limited use-by window
(commonly 7-14 days) printed on the label. Injectable formulations in
Section 4 vary in storage requirements by product; always follow the
product insert and cold-chain requirements rather than this reference for
storage decisions.

7. Explicit Out-of-Scope Questions

Consistent with AfyaPlus's safety baseline, this system does not answer
questions that ask it to choose or recommend a dose, such as:

- "What dose of [drug] should I give a [age]-year-old child?"
- "How much [drug] is safe for a patient weighing [X] kg?"
- "Is [dose] of [drug] too much for this patient?"
- "Which antibiotic should I prescribe for this presentation?"

These are clinical decisions requiring a qualified clinician's judgment,
current weight- or age-based pediatric/adult dosing references, and
patient-specific factors this system has no access to and is not designed
to evaluate. AfyaPlus responds to such questions by declining to provide a
dose recommendation rather than guessing, the same way it responds
"Information not found." to any question its knowledge base cannot
support with evidence.
